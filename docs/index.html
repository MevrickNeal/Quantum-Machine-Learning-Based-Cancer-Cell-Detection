How to Use This Tool

Three ways to check for early-stage blood cancer indicators — no medical knowledge needed

🩸

Enter Your Blood Test Values

Get a CBC (Complete Blood Count) test from your doctor — it's a routine blood test that measures 14 values like WBC, RBC, and Platelets. Enter those numbers using the sliders in the Patient Screening section below.

💡 Don't have a blood test? Click "Load High-Risk Demo" to see what a leukemia patient's values look like.

Upload a Blood Smear Image

If your doctor gave you a blood smear report or microscopy image, upload it in the Blood Cell Analysis section. The system will automatically detect and highlight each cell type — red for suspected blast cells (leukemia indicator).

💡 No image? Click "Load Demo Smear" to see a real leukemia sample with automatic cell detection.

Read Your Risk Report

The system generates a risk score (LOW / MODERATE / HIGH / CRITICAL), flags which values are abnormal, shows how many blast cells were detected, and explains which genes are most important — in plain English.

⚠ Important: Always consult a licensed hematologist. This is a research tool, not a diagnosis.

📋 What is a CBC Blood Test?

WBC (White Blood Cells)
Your immune fighters. Very high count (>11 K/µL) can indicate leukemia.
Blast Cells %
Immature blood cells. >5% is a red flag. Leukemia patients often have >20-80%.
Lymphocytes %
A type of WBC. ALL (lymphoblastic leukemia) causes these to skyrocket (>70%).
Platelets (K/µL)
Control bleeding. Leukemia often drops platelets below 50 (normal 150–400).
Hemoglobin (g/dL)
Carries oxygen. Leukemia often causes anemia — hemoglobin drops below 9 g/dL.
Neutrophils %
Infection fighters. Drop sharply in leukemia as blasts crowd out normal WBCs.

📖 Reading the Highlighted Image

Green rings = Normal Red Blood Cells (RBCs) — Tiny pink donut-shaped discs. You should have millions of these. They carry oxygen.
Cyan rings = White Blood Cells (WBCs) — Larger cells with dark purple nuclei (visible as a dark blob inside). They fight infections. Having a few is normal.
Red/Pink rings = Blast Cells (SUSPICIOUS ⚠) — Very large cells with huge irregular purple nuclei. These are immature blood cells that shouldn't be in blood. More than 5% is a warning sign for leukemia.
📖 Plain English

How the AI Models Work

No PhD needed — here's what each model actually does, explained like you're talking to a friend

⚛️
Quantum Model · Qiskit
Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)

A quantum computer learns to recognize cancer by "rotating" quantum bits (qubits) into specific positions using gene data. It's like teaching a tiny quantum spinner to lean one way for "ALL leukemia" and the other way for "AML leukemia." The word "variational" means the circuit has adjustable knobs — it trains itself by fine-tuning those knobs until it gets the right answer.

🎛️ Analogy: Like a lock combination — the quantum circuit tries different combinations until it perfectly separates the two types of leukemia.
Circuit: ZZFeatureMap (4-Qubits)
4 Qubits ZZFeatureMap COBYLA Optimizer
🔭
Quantum Model · Qiskit
Quantum Kernel SVM (QSVM)

This model uses a quantum circuit to measure how "similar" two patients' gene profiles are — called a quantum kernel. It then passes that similarity score to a classical SVM (a proven medical AI tool) to make the final decision. The quantum part makes the similarity calculation richer than any classical computer can compute.

🍎 Analogy: Imagine comparing two fingerprints through a quantum microscope — you see subtle patterns a normal eye would miss. Then a detective uses those differences to classify the suspect.
Fidelity Kernel Classical SVC Noise Tested
🧠
Quantum Model · PennyLane
Quantum Neural Network (QNN)

Works just like the AI behind ChatGPT, but instead of silicon chips, it uses quantum circuits. Gene values are converted into qubit angles (like turning a dial), passed through several "layers" of entangled qubits, and the output is a diagnosis probability. It trains by adjusting 100s of quantum gate angles using a gradient algorithm.

🎸 Analogy: Like a music equalizer — each qubit is a frequency dial. The QNN tunes all dials until the "sound" it produces perfectly matches "leukemia" or "healthy."
AngleEmbedding 3 Entangled Layers Adam Optimizer
🔍
Classical Baseline + XAI
Random Forest + SHAP Explainer

Random Forest trains 300 "decision tree" models — each one votes on whether a patient has leukemia. The majority wins. It also tells us exactly which genes triggered those votes using SHAP (a Nobel-worthy math tool from game theory). This is the most trustworthy model and currently beats the quantum models on small datasets.

⚖️ Analogy: 300 doctors examine your genes independently. They vote. Then a judge (SHAP) forces each doctor to explain exactly which clue changed their vote — so you know which genes are suspicious.
300 Trees SHAP Values ROC-AUC 1.0
🚀
The Quantum Edge
High-Dimensional Mapping

Leukemia data has 7,129 genetic features. Classical AI struggles to find complex patterns in such a massive space. Quantum ML uses Hilbert Space—a mathematical dimension much larger than normal space—to map these 7,129 variables into just 4-8 qubits. This allows the model to spot hidden "geometric shapes" in your DNA that classical computers simply cannot see.

🧬 Fact: We map gene values to "angles" on a Bloch Sphere. These angles create a unique quantum signature for each patient.
Hilbert Space DNA Mapping Bloch Sphere
🏥
Clinical Screening Model
CBC-Based Risk Classifier

Trained on 3,000 synthetic patient profiles matching published clinical distributions for healthy people, ALL, and AML patients. It learns rules like: "WBC > 50 K/µL AND Blast Cells > 20% AND Platelets < 80" = very high leukemia risk. These rules directly mirror what hematologists look for in screening.

📋 Analogy: Like a very smart triage checklist that a nurse uses in the ER — 14 blood values checked against thousands of known patient patterns, instantly.
14 CBC Features Gradient Boosting 3,000 Training Patients

Top Predictive Genes (SHAP)

How SHAP Works (Plain English)

01
What is SHAP?

SHAP stands for SHapley Additive exPlanations — a Nobel-prize-adjacent math tool that asks: "How much did THIS specific gene change the prediction?" It's like asking each gene "did YOU cause the cancer alarm?"

02
How to Read It

The longer the bar, the more important that gene is. A gene with SHAP value 0.08 made the model 8% more confident in its answer than if that gene wasn't there.

03
Clinical Meaning

High-SHAP genes are known oncogenes or tumor suppressors found in leukemia research. Cross-referencing them with published cancer gene databases validates our model.

📂 Open Data

Datasets Used in This Project

All data is publicly available — you can download and verify everything yourself

🧬
OpenML Leukemia Gene Expression

The original Golub et al. (1999) breakthrough dataset — 72 bone marrow samples from leukemia patients, with 7,129 gene expression measurements each. This was the first paper to prove AI could distinguish cancer subtypes from genes alone.

72 Samples 7,129 Genes ALL vs AML Binary Classification
OpenML Dataset #1104
📖 Source paper: Golub TR et al., "Molecular Classification of Cancer" — Science 286, 1999. This paper launched computational oncology.
🔬
MedMNIST BloodMNIST — Cell Images

17,092 microscopy images of individual blood cells (28×28px), classified into 8 cell types: Basophil, Eosinophil, Erythroblast, IG, Lymphocyte, Monocyte, Neutrophil, Platelet. Used for training the blood cell image classifier and the demo smear visualization.

17,092 Images 8 Cell Types 28×28 px RGB Microscopy
MedMNIST.com Kaggle Blood Cells Dataset
🏥
Synthetic CBC Clinical Data

3,000 synthetic patient CBC profiles generated from published clinical distribution parameters for: Healthy adults (1,500 samples), ALL patients (750), and AML patients (750). Parameters match WHO leukemia diagnostic criteria and peer-reviewed hematology references.
